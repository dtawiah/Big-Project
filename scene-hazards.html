Scene-Specific Safety Hazards

First responders must be constantly aware of potential hazards at emergency scenes. Identifying and mitigating these hazards is crucial for responder and patient safety.

General Scene Safety Principles

  • Scene size-up: Before approaching, assess the scene from a safe distance to identify potential hazards.
  • Situational awareness: Maintain continuous awareness of your surroundings and any changes in the environment.
  • Risk assessment: Evaluate the likelihood and severity of potential hazards.
  • Hazard mitigation: Take steps to eliminate or minimize hazards whenever possible.
  • Use of PPE: Wear appropriate personal protective equipment for the specific hazards present.
  • Communication: Communicate hazards to other responders and incoming units.
  • Establish control zones: Create safe work areas and restrict access to hazardous areas.
  • Follow protocols: Adhere to established safety protocols and guidelines.
  • Requesting Assistance: Know who to contact for specialized assistance in mitigating specific hazards.

Common Scene Hazards

  • Traffic Hazards:
    • Oncoming vehicles
    • Unstable vehicles
    • Road debris
    • Poor visibility
    • Bystanders
    • Who to call: Law enforcement for traffic control; Fire department for vehicle stabilization.
  • Fire Hazards:
    • Smoke inhalation
    • Structural collapse
    • Explosions
    • Hazardous materials
    • Oxygen deficiency
    • Who to call: Fire department; Hazmat team if hazardous materials are involved.
  • Electrical Hazards:
    • Downed power lines
    • Damaged electrical equipment
    • Water contact
    • Who to call: Power company; Fire department (for standby).
  • Hazardous Materials:
    • Chemical spills or leaks
    • Toxic fumes
    • Radiation
    • Biological agents
    • Who to call: Hazmat team; Environmental Protection Agency (EPA).
  • Structural Collapse:
    • Unstable buildings
    • Damaged bridges
    • Trench collapses
    • Who to call: Structural engineers; Technical Rescue Team; Utility companies (for gas/electricity).
  • Environmental Hazards:
    • Extreme weather (heat, cold, rain, snow)
    • Unstable terrain
    • Wildlife
    • Who to call: Weather service; Park rangers or animal control.
  • Violence and Crime:
    • Active shooters
    • Hostile crowds
    • Weapons
    • Unstable or combative patients
    • Who to call: Law enforcement; SWAT team if necessary.
  • Biological Hazards:
    • Infectious diseases
    • Contaminated materials
    • Who to call: Public health department; Hazmat team (if large-scale contamination).

Important Phone Numbers and Resources

Keep these numbers handy and know when to use them. This is a general guide; always follow your local protocols and procedures.

  • Emergency Dispatch (911 or local equivalent): For immediate assistance from police, fire, or ambulance.
  • Poison Control Center: For information and treatment advice related to poisoning or chemical exposure.
  • Utility Companies (Gas, Electric): To report and secure hazards related to utilities.
  • Local Law Enforcement (Non-Emergency): For situations that do not require immediate assistance but may pose a safety risk.
  • State Emergency Management Agency (SEMA): For coordinating resources during large-scale emergencies.
  • Federal Emergency Management Agency (FEMA): For federal assistance in major disasters.
